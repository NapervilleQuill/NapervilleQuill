Reporting

Performative recycling: the gap between what students are taught and what the school does

Walk down the main hallway of Naperville North High School and you will find recycling bins positioned alongside trash cans — blue for recyclables, black for waste. Posters near room 171 encourage students to recycle. Environmental science classes teach the importance of diverting waste. And yet, according to school staff and direct observation, everything ends up in the same place: the landfill.

"At school, you're often expected to learn from examples or teaching. At North, we're primed to recycle through posters and repeated presentations — but when we apply this knowledge, there is no merit."

— The Naperville Quill editorial board

This is the central contradiction at the heart of NNHS's waste management system. The infrastructure suggests a working recycling program. The reality is that recycling bins and trash bins are consolidated before pickup — a practice students and staff have observed firsthand each school day.

Every night, janitors wheel out approximately a dozen large trash bags from the building. What distinguishes this from a school that does recycle: none of those bags have been separated. Plastic cups, paper, cardboard — all headed to the same destination. One student, seen placing a banana in a bottle recycling bin each morning, may not even know the gesture is futile.

The school's waste management is handled through Groot Industries, the same service used across the city of Naperville. But whether the district has arranged for separate recycling collection — as Chicago Public Schools has done through its waste management and recycling program — remains an open question being put to district administration.

District 203

A carbon action plan with recycling on the agenda — and a $12 million deficit in the way

In March 2024, District 203's school board approved a Carbon Action Plan outlining environmental targets across waste, buildings, and energy. The plan explicitly lists color-coded recycling bins as a future goal — and as an additional cost. The irony, Quill reporters observed, is that color-coded bins are already installed throughout the building. The infrastructure exists. The system does not.

The plan sets a target year of approximately 2030 for significant progress on sustainability goals. But a $12 million budget deficit currently weighing on the district raises serious questions about the timeline for any new environmental spending — including waste separation services, staff training, and the logistics of a real recycling program.

"Recycling is what the community can be most involved in promoting."

— District 203 school board, CAP presentation

Community input collected as part of the CAP process suggests that residents and students view recycling as one of the most tangible ways to participate in the district's environmental goals. Student advisor Izzie Martinson, who was involved in the carbon emissions planning process, has been identified as a key voice on the student side of this initiative.

Among the questions the Quill is putting to district administrators: Does District 203 use the same waste service as the city? What percentage of the budget currently goes toward waste management? And are the tax benefits of certified environmental programs substantial enough to offset the upfront investment in a real recycling system?

Culture

Would students actually recycle if the school did?

One of the harder questions in this investigation is behavioral, not logistical. Even if the district arranges for separate waste collection, the bins still need to be used correctly. Trash goes in recycling. Recycling goes in trash. This is observable every single day in the NNHS cafeteria and hallways.

Is that because students don't care — or because they know it doesn't matter? The Quill's reporting suggests the latter may be the bigger factor. Students who have grown up separating waste at home arrive at school and quickly learn the unspoken rule: the bins are decorative.

AP Environmental Science students are among those who understand this disconnect most acutely. The course teaches waste diversion, landfill impacts, and recycling systems — while the building they sit in treats all of that as theoretical. APES teacher Mrs. Richardson is among those the Quill is speaking with about whether a real recycling program would shift student behavior, and whether the culture among teachers would make enforcement realistic or burdensome.

The Quill will also examine food waste — specifically, whether Naperville has local composting facilities or farms that could accept organic waste from school hot lunches, adding a second stream of diversion beyond traditional recyclables.

Editorial

We need more than a poster in room 171

Over the past several years, Naperville North has engaged in what can only be called performative recycling. The bins are there. The posters are there. The curriculum is there. What is missing is the follow-through — the actual infrastructure that would make any of it mean something.

We are not arguing that students are lazy or that administrators don't care. We are arguing that the current system removes any incentive for students to act responsibly, because acting responsibly has no effect. You can't build a culture of sustainability on a foundation that sends everything to the same landfill.

The Carbon Action Plan is a promising document. But a plan is not a program. District 203 needs to move from aspiration to implementation — and the Quill will be watching to make sure it does.
